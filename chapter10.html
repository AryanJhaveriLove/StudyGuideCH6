Chapter 10 Study Guide

Software Development: Tradeoffs and Total Costs

This chapter explains how managers participate in software projects, why requirements matter so much, and how software decisions involve tradeoffs in time, cost, scope, and organizational impact.

What to Know

This chapter is about software project management, requirements, tradeoffs, and the fact that software projects are really business projects with an IT component.

Main Theme

Successful software development depends less on coding alone and more on understanding business needs, defining clear requirements, and managing tradeoffs realistically.

Manager Takeaway

The manager’s most important role is taking responsibility for requirements and making sure the software project solves the right business problem.

Vocabulary

Requirements

Definition: Requirements are the specific things a system must do or support in order to meet business needs.

Why it matters: The slides emphasize that taking responsibility for requirements is the single most important task for future managers and users.

Example: A payroll system requirement might be that the system calculate taxes correctly under local law.

Scope

Definition: Scope is the set of features, tasks, and deliverables included in a project.

Why it matters: Expanding scope usually increases cost, time, and complexity.

Example: Adding a mobile app, dashboard, and reporting module expands project scope.

Tradeoff

Definition: A tradeoff is the need to give up one benefit in order to gain another.

Why it matters: Software projects constantly require tradeoffs among cost, features, speed, and quality.

Example: Releasing faster may mean delivering fewer features at first.

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

Definition: Total cost of ownership is the full cost of a system over its life, including purchase, implementation, training, support, upgrades, and maintenance.

Why it matters: The real cost of software is usually much larger than the initial purchase price.

Example: Buying software may be cheaper than expected upfront, but training and maintenance can make total costs much higher later.

Software Project Management

Definition: Software project management is the planning, coordination, and oversight of a software initiative from requirements through delivery.

Why it matters: Good project management keeps the system aligned with business needs and helps control cost and time.

Example: Setting milestones, tracking risks, and coordinating users and developers are project management activities.

Business Project with an IT Component

Definition: This idea means software projects should be viewed primarily as business projects that involve technology, not as isolated technical exercises.

Why it matters: The slides stress that managers must focus on business outcomes, not just technical implementation.

Example: An ERP implementation is not just an IT task; it changes operations, reporting, and employee workflows.

Stakeholder

Definition: A stakeholder is any person or group affected by the project or able to influence it.

Why it matters: Different stakeholders often have different priorities, which makes requirement gathering and project decisions harder.

Example: End users, executives, IT staff, and outside vendors can all be stakeholders.

Scope Creep

Definition: Scope creep is the gradual, often uncontrolled expansion of project scope after work has already begun.

Why it matters: Scope creep can cause delays, higher costs, and project failure.

Example: A team agrees to build a basic sales dashboard, then keeps adding analytics, mobile access, and custom reports without adjusting the timeline.

Waterfall

Definition: Waterfall is a linear development approach where stages such as planning, design, coding, testing, and deployment happen in sequence.

Why it matters: Waterfall can work when requirements are stable, but it is less flexible when requirements change.

Example: A project that finishes all planning before any coding begins is using a waterfall-style approach.

Agile

Definition: Agile is an iterative development approach that delivers work in smaller pieces and adapts as requirements become clearer.

Why it matters: Agile can help teams respond to changing needs, but it still requires clear priorities and stakeholder involvement.

Example: A team releasing new features in short cycles and adjusting based on feedback is using agile methods.

Implementation

Definition: Implementation is the process of putting a system into use within the organization.

Why it matters: Even good software can fail if implementation is poorly managed.

Example: Training users, migrating data, and switching from an old system to a new one are implementation tasks.

Change Management

Definition: Change management is the process of helping people adapt to new systems, workflows, and expectations.

Why it matters: Software projects often fail because people resist change or do not understand the new process.

Example: Training employees and explaining why a new workflow exists are parts of change management.

Risk

Definition: Risk is the possibility that something will go wrong and affect the project’s success.

Why it matters: Managers must identify and manage risks early rather than reacting after problems grow.

Example: A key vendor missing deadlines or users rejecting the system are project risks.

Customization

Definition: Customization is modifying software to fit a firm’s specific needs.

Why it matters: Customization can improve fit, but it also increases cost, complexity, and future maintenance burdens.

Example: Changing an ERP system’s workflow to match a company’s unique approval process is customization.

User Acceptance

Definition: User acceptance is the willingness of intended users to adopt and effectively use a new system.

Why it matters: A technically successful project still fails if users reject it.

Example: Employees refusing to use a new CRM system means user acceptance is low.

Key Concepts

  • Software projects should be treated as business projects, not just technical projects.
  • Clear requirements are the foundation of good software development and implementation.
  • Managers must balance time, cost, scope, and quality through tradeoffs.
  • Total cost of ownership includes much more than the purchase price of software.
  • Implementation and change management are as important as coding and design.
  • Customization can improve fit, but it also raises complexity and long-term cost.
