Chapter 2 Study Guide

Strategy and Technology: Concepts and Frameworks for Achieving Success

This chapter explains how firms create competitive advantage, how industry structure affects profit, and how technology should support strategy instead of existing for its own sake.

What to Know

This chapter is about how firms compete, how industry forces shape profitability, and how managers use strategy to build an advantage over rivals.

Main Theme

A firm can only outperform rivals if it chooses a distinct set of activities and aligns its processes and technology with that strategy.

Manager Takeaway

Managers should use technology to reinforce strategy, streamline processes, and strengthen the firm’s value chain.

Vocabulary

Strategy

Definition: Strategy is a deliberate plan for how a firm will compete and create value in a way that is different from rivals.

Why it matters: The chapter emphasizes that firms outperform rivals by choosing a different set of activities, not by simply copying competitors.

Example: Southwest Airlines built its strategy around low-cost, short-haul service with quick turnarounds and one aircraft type.

Competitive Advantage

Definition: Competitive advantage is a firm’s ability to consistently outperform its competitors.

Why it matters: Firms do not just want to survive; they want to earn better results than rivals over time.

Example: Zara’s ability to move new styles to stores quickly gives it an edge over slower fashion competitors.

Sustainable Competitive Advantage

Definition: A sustainable competitive advantage is an advantage that competitors cannot easily copy or neutralize.

Why it matters: A temporary advantage is weaker if rivals can quickly imitate it.

Example: A firm with a strong brand, efficient processes, and hard-to-copy information systems may have a sustainable advantage.

Industry Structure

Definition: Industry structure is the pattern of competition and power relationships within an industry.

Why it matters: The profitability of a firm depends partly on how attractive or difficult its industry is.

Example: An industry with many competitors and low barriers to entry is usually harder to profit in.

Porter’s Five Forces

Definition: Porter’s Five Forces is a framework for analyzing industry structure through rivalry, threat of new entrants, threat of substitutes, bargaining power of suppliers, and bargaining power of customers.

Why it matters: It helps managers understand where pressure on profits comes from.

Example: A firm in an industry with strong rivalry and low switching costs may face lower profits.

Rivalry

Definition: Rivalry is the intensity of competition among existing firms in an industry.

Why it matters: Strong rivalry makes it harder for firms to earn high profits.

Example: Used car dealers face strong rivalry because many sellers compete for the same customers.

Threat of New Entrants

Definition: The threat of new entrants is the likelihood that new firms can enter the industry and compete.

Why it matters: If entry is easy, existing firms have less protection and may earn lower profits.

Example: It is easier to start a food truck than a professional sports league team, so food trucks face a higher threat of entry.

Threat of Substitutes

Definition: The threat of substitutes is the pressure from alternative products or services that can satisfy the same customer need.

Why it matters: Substitutes limit how much a firm can charge and how secure demand will be.

Example: Rental cars may face substitutes such as ridesharing, trains, or public transit.

Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Definition: Supplier power is the ability of suppliers to demand better prices or terms from the firms that buy from them.

Why it matters: Powerful suppliers can squeeze a firm’s profits.

Example: If only a few suppliers provide a critical input, they may have strong leverage over buyers.

Bargaining Power of Customers

Definition: Customer power is the ability of buyers to pressure firms for lower prices, better quality, or better service.

Why it matters: Powerful customers reduce a firm’s ability to earn strong margins.

Example: A large automaker buying paint in huge volume has more bargaining power than an individual student buying from a bookstore.

Barriers to Entry

Definition: Barriers to entry are obstacles that make it difficult for new firms to enter an industry and compete.

Why it matters: Strong barriers to entry protect existing firms and can help preserve competitive advantage.

Example: Large startup costs, regulations, or strong brand loyalty can all be barriers to entry.

Value Chain

Definition: A value chain is the set of internal activities a firm performs to create, deliver, and support a product or service.

Why it matters: Managers can use the value chain to identify where the firm is unique and where technology can strengthen its activities.

Example: Product design, manufacturing, marketing, and service are all parts of a value chain.

Supply Chain

Definition: A supply chain is the broader network through which materials, products, and information move from suppliers to firms to customers.

Why it matters: The supply chain focuses on the external flow of goods, while the value chain focuses on internal value-creating activities.

Example: Raw materials moving from suppliers to a manufacturer and then to retailers are part of the supply chain.

Business Processes

Definition: Business processes are structured sets of activities used to produce a specific business outcome.

Why it matters: Technology should streamline and support important processes instead of being added without purpose.

Example: Order fulfillment, payroll, and customer support are business processes.

Strategic Alignment

Definition: Strategic alignment is the fit between a firm’s strategy, business processes, and information systems.

Why it matters: Technology creates more value when it supports the firm’s chosen strategy rather than existing as a separate add-on.

Example: Zara’s technology supports its fast-response model instead of being used just because it is new.

Key Concepts

  • Strategy is about being different, not just being slightly better at the same activities.
  • Competitive advantage depends on both industry structure and the firm’s internal choices.
  • Porter’s Five Forces helps managers understand where pressure on profits comes from.
  • Barriers to entry can protect firms from new competitors and support advantage.
  • The value chain focuses on internal value creation, while the supply chain focuses on the broader movement of goods and information.
  • Information systems should support strategy by strengthening processes and value chain activities.
