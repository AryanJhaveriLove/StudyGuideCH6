Chapter 4 Study Guide

Disruptive Technologies: Understanding How New Technologies Change Markets

This chapter explains what truly disruptive innovation is, how it differs from sustaining innovation, and why established firms often fail to respond effectively when new technologies first appear weak or unimportant.

What to Know

This chapter is about why some new technologies reshape markets and why dominant firms often miss the threat until it is too late.

Main Theme

Disruptive innovations usually start out worse on the dimensions current customers care about, but improve over time and eventually invade mainstream markets.

Manager Takeaway

Managers must recognize that early weak performance does not mean a technology is unimportant. Sometimes the real threat is hidden in affordability, accessibility, and future improvement.

Vocabulary

Disruptive Innovation

Definition: Disruptive innovation is a process in which a new technology or business model begins by serving low-end, overlooked, or entirely new customers and later improves enough to challenge established firms in the mainstream market.

Why it matters: Managers often ignore disruptive technologies because they initially seem weaker or less profitable than existing products.

Example: Early digital cameras had poor image quality, but over time they improved enough to replace film cameras for most consumers.

Sustaining Innovation

Definition: Sustaining innovation improves existing products or services along dimensions that current customers already value.

Why it matters: Established firms are usually good at sustaining innovation because it matches the demands of their current customers and business models.

Example: A new smartphone model with a better camera, faster processor, and longer battery life is usually a sustaining innovation.

Enabling Technology

Definition: An enabling technology is a technology that makes a product or service more affordable, accessible, or practical for a wider group of users.

Why it matters: Many successful disruptive technologies depend on enabling technology to reach customers who were previously excluded from a market.

Example: GPUs helped enable modern generative AI by making large-scale model training and inference possible.

Value Network

Definition: A value network is the set of suppliers, partners, distributors, customers, and complementary relationships that support a business model or technology.

Why it matters: A disruptive technology succeeds more easily when the broader network benefits from its growth.

Example: Cars were supported by a broader value network that included roads, gas stations, mechanics, and manufacturers.

Innovative Business Model

Definition: An innovative business model is a new way of delivering and monetizing a product or service that changes who can access it and how value is captured.

Why it matters: Disruption is often driven not just by technology, but also by a business model that targets new users or low-end customers more effectively.

Example: Ride-sharing apps offered a different business model than traditional taxi companies by using smartphones and flexible drivers.

Market Shock

Definition: A market shock is a sudden and significant change that disrupts existing competition and customer behavior.

Why it matters: Truly disruptive technologies can create shocks that force firms to rethink strategy and operations.

Example: The shift from physical media to streaming changed how entertainment firms made money and reached customers.

Low-End Market

Definition: The low-end market consists of customers who are overserved by existing products and may accept a cheaper, simpler alternative.

Why it matters: Disruptive innovations often begin by appealing to low-end customers that leading firms do not prioritize.

Example: A simpler service with lower performance but much lower price may appeal to customers who do not need premium features.

New Market Disruption

Definition: New market disruption occurs when a product or service creates a new market by reaching customers who previously did not consume the traditional product.

Why it matters: Incumbents may ignore these markets because they do not resemble their current customers.

Example: Early personal computing opened computing to users who would never have bought a mainframe or minicomputer.

Price Elasticity of Technology

Definition: Price elasticity of technology refers to the idea that as technology becomes cheaper, demand and usage often increase dramatically.

Why it matters: Falling prices can turn a weak early technology into a major competitive threat once it becomes affordable to mass users.

Example: As digital storage and bandwidth became cheaper, streaming services became practical at large scale.

Incumbent Firm

Definition: An incumbent firm is an established company already dominant or active in an industry.

Why it matters: Incumbents often miss disruptive threats because they focus on satisfying current customers and protecting current margins.

Example: A dominant film camera company was an incumbent when digital photography began to emerge.

Technology Adoption Curve

Definition: The technology adoption curve describes how different groups of customers adopt innovations at different times, from early adopters to the mainstream market.

Why it matters: Disruptive innovations often begin with fringe or niche users before eventually improving enough for the mainstream.

Example: New AI tools may be adopted first by technical users before broader business adoption follows.

Platform Envelopment

Definition: Platform envelopment occurs when one platform enters another market by bundling overlapping functionality into its existing offering.

Why it matters: A dominant platform can quickly overpower smaller standalone firms by using its current user base and scope.

Example: A large social media app adding built-in photo editing or marketplace tools can threaten specialized standalone firms.

Analog to Digital Shift

Definition: The analog to digital shift is the transition from physical or analog technologies to digital ones.

Why it matters: Many classic disruptive innovations began as weak digital alternatives before eventually outperforming older analog systems on the dimensions that mattered most.

Example: Music moved from physical CDs to digital files and then to streaming platforms.

Key Concepts

  • Disruptive innovation is not just any big innovation. It usually starts weaker on traditional performance measures.
  • Sustaining innovation improves what current customers already value, while disruptive innovation often begins outside the mainstream.
  • Established firms often ignore disruptive technologies because their current customers do not initially want them.
  • Affordability, accessibility, and business model innovation often matter as much as raw technical performance.
  • A disruptive technology becomes more dangerous as it improves and moves into the mainstream market.
  • Managers should pay attention to technologies that seem weak now but could improve rapidly over time.

Interactive Practice Quiz

1. An established camera company ignores early digital cameras because the image quality is much worse than film and its best customers are not asking for digital products. 2. A leading software company keeps adding new premium features to satisfy its current enterprise customers. 3. A company argues that a new technology can never become important because it currently performs worse than the incumbent product on the metric most established customers care about.
