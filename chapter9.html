Chapter 9 Study Guide

Why Buy When You Can Rent? Cloud Computing and Its Commercialization

This chapter explains how cloud computing lets firms rent computing resources over the internet instead of owning infrastructure, and why that shift changes cost, flexibility, scalability, and strategy.

What to Know

This chapter is about cloud computing, utility computing, and the business consequences of renting technology resources instead of buying them outright.

Main Theme

Cloud computing changes IT from something firms must own and manage themselves into something they can access on demand as a service.

Manager Takeaway

Managers need to weigh flexibility, scalability, cost, downtime risk, and vendor dependence when deciding how much of the firm’s computing should move to the cloud.

Vocabulary

Cloud Computing

Definition: Cloud computing is the delivery of computing resources such as storage, software, processing power, and platforms over the internet.

Why it matters: Firms can access technology without buying and maintaining all of the underlying infrastructure themselves.

Example: A company using Amazon Web Services instead of building its own server room is using cloud computing.

Utility Computing

Definition: Utility computing is the idea that computing resources can be consumed like a utility, with users paying for what they use rather than owning the infrastructure.

Why it matters: This changes IT from a fixed capital expense into something that can be consumed more flexibly.

Example: A startup paying monthly for cloud storage rather than buying servers upfront is using utility computing logic.

Software as a Service (SaaS)

Definition: SaaS is software delivered over the internet rather than installed and run primarily on a local machine.

Why it matters: SaaS reduces installation burden and often makes updates and collaboration easier.

Example: Google Docs and Salesforce are SaaS examples.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

Definition: IaaS is the delivery of basic computing infrastructure, such as servers, storage, and networking, as a service.

Why it matters: Firms can rent infrastructure instead of owning and maintaining physical hardware.

Example: Renting virtual servers from a cloud provider is IaaS.

Platform as a Service (PaaS)

Definition: PaaS is a cloud model in which developers rent a platform for building, testing, and deploying applications.

Why it matters: It reduces the need for firms to manage underlying infrastructure while still supporting software development.

Example: A developer using a managed cloud platform to deploy web apps is using PaaS.

Scalability

Definition: Scalability is the ability of a system to handle growth in demand without breaking down.

Why it matters: Cloud systems can often scale up or down more easily than traditional owned infrastructure.

Example: An online retailer increasing server capacity during holiday demand is using scalable infrastructure.

Elasticity

Definition: Elasticity is the ability of computing resources to expand or contract quickly in response to changes in demand.

Why it matters: Elasticity lets firms avoid paying for peak capacity all the time.

Example: A site automatically adding resources during traffic spikes and reducing them later shows elasticity.

Downtime

Definition: Downtime is a period when a system or service is unavailable.

Why it matters: Cloud providers can offer strong reliability, but outages still matter because many firms depend on them.

Example: If a cloud platform goes down, customers may lose access to business applications temporarily.

Vendor Lock-In

Definition: Vendor lock-in occurs when it becomes difficult or costly to switch from one cloud provider or service platform to another.

Why it matters: Cloud convenience can create long-term dependence if systems, data, and workflows become tied to one provider.

Example: A company whose apps, storage, and tools are deeply integrated with one provider may struggle to move elsewhere.

Capital Expenditure (CapEx)

Definition: CapEx is money spent upfront on long-term assets such as servers, buildings, or equipment.

Why it matters: Cloud computing can reduce the need for large upfront capital investments in IT infrastructure.

Example: Building a private data center requires major capital expenditure.

Operating Expenditure (OpEx)

Definition: OpEx is spending on ongoing operations and recurring services rather than long-term owned assets.

Why it matters: Cloud services often shift IT costs from CapEx to OpEx.

Example: Paying a monthly cloud bill is an operating expense.

On-Demand Self-Service

Definition: On-demand self-service means users can access computing resources when needed without direct human intervention from the provider.

Why it matters: It increases speed and flexibility for firms that need resources quickly.

Example: A firm launching additional storage capacity from an online dashboard is using on-demand self-service.

Key Concepts

  • Cloud computing changes IT from owned infrastructure to rented services.
  • SaaS, IaaS, and PaaS are different layers of cloud service.
  • Cloud computing improves flexibility, scalability, and speed of deployment.
  • Firms often shift spending from large upfront CapEx to recurring OpEx when adopting cloud services.
  • Cloud benefits come with risks such as downtime and vendor lock-in.
