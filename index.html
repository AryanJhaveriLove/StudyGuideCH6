Chapter 6 Study Guide

Moore's Law and More: Fast/Cheap Computing and What This Means for the Manager

This study guide includes precise vocabulary, clear explanations, and a 5-question multiple choice quiz for review.

What to Know

This chapter is about how fast computing improves, why it gets cheaper over time, and how managers should think about the business impact of those changes.

Main Theme

Technology improves exponentially, not linearly. When computing becomes cheaper, companies discover new uses for it and entire industries can change.

Manager Takeaway

Managers need to ask: What becomes possible when computing is dramatically faster and cheaper?

Vocabulary

Moore's Law

Definition: Moore's Law states that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every 18 to 24 months, leading to exponential growth in computing power.

Why it matters: It helps explain why technology becomes faster and cheaper over time.

Example: A smartphone today is more powerful than many older high-end computers.

Exponential Growth

Definition: Exponential growth is growth that increases at an accelerating rate rather than by the same amount each period.

Why it matters: Managers often underestimate how quickly technological capability can improve.

Example: AI performance improving very rapidly over a short period of time.

Price Elasticity of Demand

Definition: Price elasticity of demand measures how strongly demand changes when price changes. In technology markets, demand is often highly elastic.

Why it matters: When computing gets cheaper, people want more of it and discover new uses for it.

Example: As cloud storage prices fell, businesses and consumers stored far more data online.

Falling Cost of Computing

Definition: The falling cost of computing refers to the continued decline in the cost of processing power, storage, and network capacity.

Why it matters: Lower costs make new business models and products possible.

Example: Streaming platforms became practical because storing and delivering large media files became more affordable.

Disruptive Innovation

Definition: Disruptive innovation occurs when a new technology or business model begins in a weaker or simpler form and eventually changes or replaces established markets.

Why it matters: Cheap computing often enables disruptive innovations that challenge established firms.

Example: Generative AI is changing how customer service, marketing, and content creation are performed.

Digital Convergence

Definition: Digital convergence is the merging of multiple technologies or devices into one product or platform.

Why it matters: It reduces the need for separate devices and creates more value for users.

Example: Smartphones combine a camera, GPS, music player, web browser, and phone into one device.

Computationally Intensive Applications

Definition: Computationally intensive applications are programs that require large amounts of processing power to function.

Why it matters: These applications become more practical as computing power increases and costs fall.

Example: Machine learning models and generative AI systems require substantial computing resources.

Managerial Blind Spot

Definition: A managerial blind spot occurs when a manager fails to recognize the speed or importance of technological change.

Why it matters: This can cause firms to miss opportunities or respond too slowly to disruption.

Example: A firm ignoring artificial intelligence until competitors have already adopted it at scale.

Key Concepts

  • Computing power tends to improve exponentially rather than linearly.
  • As the price of computing falls, businesses and consumers find more ways to use it.
  • Cheaper computing can enable new industries, products, and business models.
  • Managers must think ahead instead of assuming technology will only improve slowly.
  • Fast, cheap computing can create major competitive threats and opportunities.

Chapter 7 Study Guide

Understanding Software: A Primer for Managers

This chapter focuses on how managers should classify software, understand software layers, and connect software choices to lock-in, usability, and business value.

Main Framework

Ask four questions: What does the software do? Who can change the source code? Where does the code run? What standards does it use?

Big Manager Idea

Managers do not need to code every system, but they do need to understand what kind of software they are buying, using, or depending on.

Why It Matters

Software choices influence flexibility, compatibility, lock-in, cost, user experience, and how easily a business can scale.

Chapter 7 Vocabulary

Software

Definition: Software is a set of instructions that tells hardware what to do.

Why it matters: Managers make decisions about software constantly, even if they never write code themselves.

Example: Microsoft Excel, Windows, and Salesforce are all software, but they serve different roles.

Operating System (OS)

Definition: An operating system is the core software layer that manages hardware resources and provides services that other software depends on.

Why it matters: The OS makes computers usable for both programmers and end users and shapes which other applications can run.

Example: Windows, macOS, Linux, and iOS are operating systems.

Database Management System (DBMS)

Definition: A DBMS is software used to store, organize, retrieve, and manage large amounts of structured data.

Why it matters: Businesses rely on DBMS tools to support applications, reporting, and decision-making.

Example: MySQL and Oracle Database are DBMS products.

User Application

Definition: A user application is software designed to help end users perform specific tasks.

Why it matters: This is the software people interact with directly, so it strongly affects productivity and user experience.

Example: Excel, Google Docs, and Photoshop are user applications.

Closed Source (Proprietary) Software

Definition: Closed source software is software whose source code can be modified only by the company or organization that owns it.

Why it matters: Proprietary software can give owners more control, but it can also increase dependency on the vendor.

Example: Microsoft Excel is proprietary software.

Open Source Software

Definition: Open source software is software whose source code is available for others to inspect, modify, and redistribute.

Why it matters: Open source can increase flexibility and lower dependency on a single vendor.

Example: Linux is open source software.

Local Software

Definition: Local software runs on the user’s own machine and does not require a network connection to execute its core functions.

Why it matters: Local software can offer speed and offline access, but it may be harder to update or share across many users.

Example: A desktop version of Excel installed on a laptop is local software.

Hosted Software

Definition: Hosted software runs on a remote server rather than on the user’s device and is accessed through a browser or client.

Why it matters: Hosted software can simplify collaboration and updates, but it creates dependence on networks and service providers.

Example: Google Sheets is hosted software.

Open Standard

Definition: An open standard allows other companies to develop compatible products without needing special permission from the owner.

Why it matters: Open standards often support broader ecosystems and compatibility.

Example: Microsoft software often works within relatively open standards compared with closed Apple-only complements.

Closed Standard

Definition: A closed standard requires permission from the owner before other firms can develop complementary products or compatible software.

Why it matters: Closed standards can give a firm tighter control over quality and profits, but they may also limit ecosystem growth.

Example: Apple’s ecosystem is often used as an example of a more closed standard.

Konana’s Ecosystem Model

Definition: Konana’s Ecosystem Model shows how software and hardware exist in layered dependencies, from hardware up through operating systems, databases, middleware, and applications.

Why it matters: Because each layer depends on the one below it, choices at one layer can create switching costs and lock-in across the whole stack.

Example: Choosing a specific operating system can influence what applications and complementary products a firm can use.

Lock-in

Definition: Lock-in occurs when a user or firm becomes dependent on a product or ecosystem and faces difficulty switching away.

Why it matters: Lock-in can protect vendors and make switching costly for customers.

Example: A company that builds many workflows around one software stack may find switching expensive and disruptive.

Graphical User Interface (GUI)

Definition: A GUI is a visual interface that allows users to interact with software through windows, icons, menus, buttons, and other on-screen elements instead of only command-line text.

Why it matters: GUIs made computers much easier for non-programmers to use.

Example: Drop-down menus, check boxes, and icons are all parts of a GUI.

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP)

Definition: ERP is integrated software that connects multiple business functions, such as accounting, inventory, HR, and operations, into one shared system.

Why it matters: ERP software can improve coordination across a business, but it also affects many processes and departments at once.

Example: A company using one system to manage orders, inventory, payroll, and reporting is using ERP.

CRM, SCM, and BI Systems

Definition: CRM systems manage customer relationships, SCM systems support supply chain activities, and BI systems analyze data for decision-making.

Why it matters: Managers should understand how different categories of software support different business goals.

Example: Salesforce is a CRM example, while a reporting dashboard used by executives is a BI example.

Key Concepts

  • Managers should classify software using four questions: function, source code ownership, execution location, and standards.
  • Software exists in layers, and lower-layer choices affect higher-layer choices.
  • Open versus closed approaches affect compatibility, control, and ecosystem growth.
  • Local versus hosted software changes cost structure, convenience, and dependency on networks.
  • GUIs expanded computer use by making systems accessible to non-programmers.
  • Enterprise software such as ERP, CRM, SCM, and BI can influence major business processes and coordination.
